**“The Clockmaker’s Promise”**

In a quiet town where every house leaned slightly from age, there lived an old clockmaker named Herrin. His shop smelled of cedar and brass, and every clock inside—whether grand or small—ticked in perfect harmony, as if they shared a single heartbeat.

One misty morning, a little girl named Lysa entered the shop clutching a cracked, silent pocket watch.

“It was my father’s,” she said. “Can you make it tick again?”

Herrin studied the watch. Its gears were worn, its spring nearly dust. But he saw the determination in Lysa’s eyes—the kind only love could shape. He nodded.

For days he worked. He replaced gears as delicately as if handling bird bones, polished the casing until it caught the faint winter sun, and whispered to the watch as if reminding it how to breathe.

On the fifth evening, just as the lamplight flickered out, the watch gave a faint *tick*.

Herrin smiled. A promise kept.

When Lysa returned, he placed the watch in her palms. It ticked steadily—alive again. She looked up at him with such gratitude that it warmed the cold shop.

But as she left, Herrin noticed something: her steps matched the rhythm of the watch, soft and certain. And he realized then what he had really repaired.

Not the watch.

Her hope.
